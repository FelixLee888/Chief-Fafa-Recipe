Ingredients

  • Ten cans of abalone, just right
  • Abalone sauce:
  • Abalone water (the juice soaking the abalone in the can) one full can
  • Japanese sake 300 ml
  • Oyster sauce 90 g
  • Japanese soy sauce 80 ml
  • Do NOT casually pour away the water soaking the abalone
  • Mirin 50 ml
  • Sugar 35 g
  • Sesame oil 1 tablespoon
  • Water 300 ml
  • Preparation:
  • Only one thing needs to be prepared in advance, which is to make several dense horizontal and vertical cuts on the bottom of the abalone. This is not for appearance, because canned abalone is already cooked and will not "flower" during simmering, but to help the abalone absorb flavor faster. Also, be gentle when cutting; splitting the abalone in half is minor, but cutting your hand is a big problem.

Instructions

  1. First, pour the sake and an equal amount of water into a heavy pot, bring to a boil over high heat until the sharp alcohol smell disappears;
  2. In order, add the abalone water, soy sauce, mirin, sugar, oyster sauce, and sesame oil all into the pot, then bring to a full boil over high heat (be careful of boiling over!);
  3. Put the abalone into the boiling sauce, bring to a full boil again, then reduce to the lowest heat to continue simmering;
  4. Simmer for about half an hour until the room smells of abalone, and by one hour the sauce will have reduced to about half. At this time, the abalone color will darken, indicating it is done;
  5. Remove the abalone. For hot serving, eat immediately. For cold serving, let cool, drizzle a little sauce on top, seal with cling film, and refrigerate for three hours before eating;
  6. Do not discard the leftover sauce from cooking the abalone. As you know, it is the essence and flavor. It can be used to braise shiitake mushrooms and goose feet, or simply to toss noodles, which is also delicious. Seal it in a vacuum bag and freeze; it can be kept for several weeks without problem.
  7. This time I found out I ran out of oyster sauce at home, so I just added a bit more soy sauce and sugar to enhance the flavor, which is fine. At the end of cooking, you can add a little dark soy sauce to make the abalone look more flavorful (yes, just "look" more flavorful, but sometimes people are superficial and associate darker food with stronger taste). You can also add some spirits like cognac or brandy to add aroma variation. I have a lot of whiskey at home, so I added whiskey, which works just as well.
  8. Although this method is simple, the abalone texture is soft, tender, and rich in abalone aroma. Most importantly, the flavor is rich but not greasy, guaranteed to keep you eating. Some friends asked if fresh abalone can be used. Personally, fresh abalone becomes tough when heated, and this long simmering method may not achieve such good texture. Also, the abalone water in canned abalone is a key ingredient in this snack. If using fresh abalone, you can substitute chicken broth, but the abalone flavor will not be as strong.
